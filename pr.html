PR & Media Strategy Framework

Master the key elements of successful PR campaigns for B2B SaaS

  1. Develop Your Newsworthy Angle

    Create stories that journalists actually want to cover

    • Focus on real achievements (product launches, milestones, funding)
    • Generate original data or research about your industry
    • Connect your story to current trends or news cycles
    • Tell a unique founder story or company journey
    • Showcase unusual customer success metrics or case studies

  2. Build Your Media List

    Identify and research the right journalists and outlets

    • Research publications your target customers actually read
    • Find journalists who cover your specific niche or industry
    • Follow target journalists on social media to understand their interests
    • Create tiered media lists (top tier publications vs. industry blogs)
    • Consider podcasts, YouTube channels, and newsletters, not just traditional media

  3. Craft Perfect Pitches

    Create compelling pitches that get responses

    • Keep pitches short, specific, and personalized to each journalist
    • Lead with a compelling hook that aligns with their beat
    • Include concrete data points, not just generic claims
    • Offer exclusive angles or first access when appropriate
    • Make it easy for them to cover by providing all necessary resources

  4. Build Relationships

    Develop ongoing relationships with media contacts

    • Engage with journalists' content before pitching them
    • Offer genuine expertise and insights, not just promotion
    • Respect their time and deadlines
    • Provide rapid responses to media inquiries
    • Be a reliable source by delivering on promises

  5. Amplify Coverage

    Maximize the impact of earned media

    • Share coverage across all company channels (social, email, etc.)
    • Create content snippets from larger pieces for social media
    • Add media logos to your website's "As Seen In" section
    • Repurpose coverage in sales enablement materials
    • Track and measure the impact of each media mention

Frequently Asked Questions

Do B2B SaaS startups really need PR, or should we focus on other channels first?

While PR shouldn't necessarily be your first marketing channel (direct customer acquisition channels often deserve priority), it can play a valuable complementary role even for early-stage startups. PR is particularly useful when: 1) You've achieved product-market fit and have genuine customer success stories, 2) You're entering a crowded market and need to establish credibility, 3) Your target customers heavily rely on industry publications for buying decisions, or 4) You have a truly innovative approach that warrants attention. The key is timing—PR works best when you have something genuinely newsworthy to share and the infrastructure to capitalize on the attention it generates. Many successful SaaS companies use PR strategically at key moments (like product launches or funding rounds) while focusing on more predictable channels for day-to-day growth.

Should we hire a PR agency or handle media relations in-house?

This depends on your specific situation, budget, and goals. In-house PR can work well when: 1) You have team members with media relations experience, 2) You're targeting a very specialized niche where deep product knowledge is crucial, 3) You're on a limited budget and can dedicate time to building relationships, or 4) You're taking a long-term approach to thought leadership. PR agencies become valuable when: 1) You need immediate access to established media relationships, 2) You're planning a major launch or announcement, 3) You lack the internal expertise or bandwidth for consistent outreach, or 4) You need specialized expertise in crisis management or analyst relations. Many successful SaaS companies use a hybrid approach—handling ongoing thought leadership internally while engaging agencies for specific campaigns or to access certain media relationships.

How can we generate newsworthy angles when we don't have major funding or product announcements?

There are several approaches for creating media-worthy stories between major announcements: 1) Original Research or Data - Analyze anonymized customer data to identify interesting trends or commission a survey on a relevant industry topic, 2) Customer Success Stories - Feature impressive results or unusual applications of your product, 3) Executive Thought Leadership - Offer contrarian or forward-looking perspectives on industry developments, 4) Newsjacking - Provide expert commentary on breaking industry news, 5) Milestone Announcements - Celebrate achieving X customers or Y in annual recurring revenue, 6) Company Culture Stories - Highlight unique workplace policies or social impact initiatives. The key is finding the intersection between what journalists find interesting and what authentically represents your brand. Focus on stories that provide genuine value to the publication's audience rather than purely promotional angles.

How do we measure the ROI of PR efforts?

Measuring PR ROI requires a multi-layered approach: 1) Direct Attribution - Use UTM parameters for coverage links and create specific landing pages for PR campaigns to track resulting traffic, sign-ups, and conversions, 2) Brand Impact - Run brand awareness and sentiment surveys before and after major PR pushes, 3) Sales Influence - Add "How did you hear about us?" fields in forms and track mentions of media coverage in sales conversations, 4) SEO Value - Calculate the equivalent cost of acquiring backlinks from similar domains through other means, 5) Long-term Impact - Measure improvements in sales cycle length or conversion rates when press coverage is mentioned during the process. While PR's full impact is sometimes difficult to quantify in the short term, implementing these tracking mechanisms can help demonstrate value. The most sophisticated companies use attribution modeling that accounts for PR's supporting role in the broader customer journey rather than expecting it to drive conversions in isolation.

How frequently should we be reaching out to media contacts?

Quality and relevance always trump frequency when it comes to media outreach. Rather than adhering to a rigid schedule, focus on: 1) Genuine News Value - Only reach out when you have something truly valuable and relevant to the journalist's beat, 2) Relationship Building - Engage with journalists' content and provide helpful insights even when you're not pitching, 3) Publication Cycles - Understand the editorial calendars and news cycles of target publications, 4) Personalization - Tailor each pitch specifically to the journalist rather than sending mass emails. As a general guideline, most journalists prefer receiving pitches from the same source no more than once every 4-6 weeks unless there's breaking news. Building a diverse media list allows you to maintain visibility without overwhelming individual contacts. Remember that one perfectly timed, highly relevant pitch is worth dozens of generic ones—respect journalists' inboxes and you'll see better long-term results.
