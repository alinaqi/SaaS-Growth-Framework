The Infrastructure

Setting up the foundation for effective cold outbound campaigns

If the world's best cold email is sent but it never reaches the recipient's primary inbox, the email is worthless. Infrastructure is a prerequisite for good outbound - you might succeed with mediocre emails and great infrastructure, but you'll never succeed with great emails if your infrastructure doesn't work.

  1. Buy and Set Up Adjacent Domains

    Purchase domains similar to your company's main domain to ensure deliverability at scale

    • Buy domains that are similar to your company's main domain (e.g., getcompany.com, withcompany.com)
    • Redirect these domains to your main website
    • Calculate how many domains you need based on your desired email volume
    • Use the formula: Adjacent Domains = (Desired Daily Volume / 60) x 1.1

  2. Optimize Your Domains for Deliverability

    Set up authentication records to boost your domain authority with email service providers

    • Set up SPF records to specify which mail servers can send email on behalf of your domain
    • Configure DKIM to add a digital signature that verifies your emails haven't been tampered with
    • Implement DMARC to tell receiving servers how to handle authentication failures
    • These steps boost your authenticity in the eyes of email service providers like Gmail

  3. Create Email Inboxes on Your Domains

    Set up the right infrastructure for sending emails from your domains

    • Set up 3 inboxes per domain to reduce risk and diversify sending
    • Use Google Workspace or Microsoft 365 (avoid private IP solutions)
    • Create separate Google Workspace accounts for different domains (2 domains per account max)
    • Use VPNs and antidetect browsers when setting up multiple accounts
    • Use real human names for your email accounts, preferably company leaders

  4. Warm Up Your Email Inboxes

    Establish a positive reputation for your new email accounts before sending outbound

    • Send emails from your accounts to other email accounts that engage positively
    • Create a profile of activity matching what a normal good email inbox looks like
    • Warm from the same IP you'll be sending outbound from
    • Start with lower volume and gradually increase over several weeks
    • Continue warming activities even after you start sending cold outbound

Why Skip This At Your Peril

Many outbound campaigns fail at the infrastructure stage. Most companies use a single inbox per person approach, which is inefficient and risky:

  • ESPs want to see human-like volume from email accounts (not hundreds of identical emails)
  • Sending from your main domain puts your entire company's email reputation at risk
  • Without proper authentication records, your emails are more likely to be flagged as spam
  • When you're sending at scale, diversifying across domains protects your deliverability

Writing Great Emails

Crafting cold emails that get positive, revenue-generating replies

Deciding Who To Email

Focus on finding people who are actively feeling the pain your product solves:

  • Ask: "Who is currently feeling the pain that our product solves?"
  • Look for signals that indicate pain (hiring patterns, tools used, etc.)
  • For small companies (<50 people), email the founder directly
  • For larger companies, target decision-makers and higher-ups
  • Email multiple people at the same company when appropriate

Getting The Ideas Right

Focus your email on addressing one of these two key questions:

  • How will you make their company more money?
  • How will you make this specific person's life easier/better?
  • Use debate-style contentions to strengthen your arguments
  • Structure with Claim, Warrants, Evidence, and Impact
  • Make sure each argument is solid before including it in your email

Elements of Great Cold Emails

Most effective cold emails include these components:

  • Personalization: Something that makes the email feel 1:1 and not mass-sent
  • Strong claim: A compelling statement about the value you provide
  • Evidence: Proof points that back up your claim
  • Clear next step: A specific action for the recipient to take
  • Brevity: Keep it under 200 words to respect their time

Example Cold Email That Worked

Hey [Name] — Congrats on the recent raise (Lachy Groom also funded a previous company of mine, was generally helpful). Happy to see that [Company] looks a bit different from all the generic/gimmicky AI support startups out there. Looked at your job board and noticed you're hiring for sales. Imagine you're planning to use outbound to gain significant early/high ROI traction? If so - you should check out Za-zu, my company. It's an email sequencer that lets one smart growth person send personalized outbound at the scale of 50 SDRs. I'd love to show you the product. Would it be crazy to chat this week? Matt

Nailing the Style, Tone, and Jargon

The style of your email matters as much as the content:

  • Adjust your tone based on your audience (more casual for startups, more formal for enterprise)
  • Don't try to fake a casual tone with unprofessional language
  • Test a slightly more casual tone than you think is needed
  • Learn and use industry-specific jargon correctly
  • Refer to companies the way insiders do
  • Write subject lines that feel like internal emails
  • Use a profile picture to increase open rates

Sending Personalized Outbound at Scale

Using AI to automate highly effective, personalized cold emails

AI-Powered Personalization

Use AI to fill in the personalized elements of your emails at scale:

  1. Write a dream 1:1, perfectly personalized email
  2. Identify the elements that need personalization
  3. Train AI to replace those elements with personalized content
  4. Test extensively to ensure quality before launching

AI can handle different levels of personalization:

  • Simple copy and paste (first name, company name)
  • Summarization and normalization of data
  • Conditional content based on if-then logic
  • More complex personalized elements like industry-specific insights

Campaign Structure

Effective campaigns have a thoughtful multi-stage structure:

  • Create 4-6 stages (individual emails in sequence)
  • Write 3-6 variants for each stage to test different approaches
  • Space emails appropriately (2 days, 4 days, 7 days, etc.)
  • Create a narrative arc across the entire campaign
  • Use different messaging angles in different variants
  • Implement spintaxing to avoid identical emails

Prompting AI Effectively

Get high-quality personalization from AI with these techniques:

Instructions

  • Start with clear input data for AI to work with
  • Write concise, specific instructions
  • Include helpful Dos and Don'ts to guide output
  • Test repeatedly and refine your prompts

Examples

  • Provide input-output pairs as examples
  • Include multiple positive examples
  • Show both the input data and the desired output
  • Create a persona for the AI to adopt

Optimizing Based on Data

Use campaign performance data to iteratively improve your results:

  • Track open rates and reply rates across stages and variants
  • Classify replies as positive or negative
  • When a variant significantly outperforms others, redirect volume to it
  • Create new variants based on what's working best
  • Test one variable at a time to identify what's driving success
  • Be willing to completely rethink your messaging if data suggests it

Frequently Asked Questions

How many emails should I send per day?

It depends on your infrastructure setup. With the approach described in this handbook, we recommend sending about 20 emails per day per inbox. With 3 inboxes per domain, that's 60 emails per domain per day. Scale your operation by adding more domains rather than increasing the send rate per inbox. This approach mimics natural human sending patterns and reduces the risk of being flagged as spam.

How long should I warm my email accounts before sending cold outbound?

You should warm your email accounts for at least 3-4 weeks before starting cold outbound campaigns. The warming process should start slowly and gradually increase to your target volume. Continue warming activities even after you begin sending cold outbound to maintain a healthy sender reputation. The key is to create a pattern of engagement that matches what a normal, well-received email account would look like.

What's the ideal cold email length?

The ideal cold email should be under 200 words. Shorter emails tend to perform better as they respect the recipient's time and are more likely to be read completely. Focus on making every word count - include personalization, a strong value proposition, brief evidence, and a clear call to action. Avoid lengthy introductions or detailed explanations that can be saved for follow-up conversations.

How do I avoid being flagged as spam?

To avoid being flagged as spam: 1) Set up proper authentication records (SPF, DKIM, DMARC), 2) Warm up your email accounts properly, 3) Maintain human-like sending patterns and volume, 4) Personalize your emails genuinely, 5) Use multiple domains to diversify risk, 6) Avoid spam trigger words and excessive punctuation, 7) Test your emails before large sends, and 8) Monitor your deliverability and engagement metrics consistently. If you see signs of deliverability issues, pause sending and address them immediately.

What makes a good subject line for cold emails?

Good cold email subject lines feel like they could be the subject line of an internal email - natural and not overly marketing-focused. Examples include "Quick question," "Idea for [specific area]," or "[Mutual connection] suggested I reach out." Avoid clickbait, ALL CAPS, excessive punctuation, or overtly salesy language. The goal is to get your email opened, not to immediately sell something. A/B test different subject lines to see what works best for your specific audience.

How many follow-up emails should I send?

A good cold email sequence typically includes 4-6 emails sent over the course of 2-4 weeks. The first follow-up should be sent 2-3 days after the initial email, with subsequent follow-ups spaced further apart (4-7 days, then 7-14 days). Each follow-up should add value and not just ask "did you see my last email?" The final email can be a "break-up" email letting them know you won't be reaching out again, which often generates replies from people who were interested but busy.

What's the difference between "warming" and "ramping up"?

Warming refers to establishing a positive reputation for your email account by generating engagement (opens, replies, etc.) before sending cold outbound. Ramping up refers to gradually increasing the volume of cold emails you send. Both are important but serve different purposes. Warming focuses on quality signals from engagement, while ramping up focuses on managing quantity to avoid sending red flags to email service providers. A properly warmed inbox should still be ramped up gradually when starting cold outbound campaigns.

How personalized do my emails really need to be?

True personalization is crucial for effective cold emails. Generic "personalization" like "I saw your LinkedIn post" no longer works as recipients have grown wise to mail-merge tactics. Your emails should include elements that clearly show you've done research on the recipient and their company. This could be mentioning specific pain points their company is likely facing, referencing recent company news, or noting their specific role in addressing the problem you solve. With AI, you can achieve deep personalization at scale by identifying patterns in your data and training AI to craft tailored messages for each recipient.
